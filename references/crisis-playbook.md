# Crisis & Regime-Shock Playbook — Survive the Collapse, Hunt the Generational Trade

Most of the desk's rules assume a *normal* tape. A **crisis** is not a normal tape: correlations
go to 1 (diversification stops working), liquidity evaporates, stops gap through their levels,
and the crowd sells indiscriminately — good businesses and zombies together. Two things are true
at once, and the desk must hold both:

1. **A crisis is the fastest way to be permanently impaired** — leverage, illiquidity, and
   catching falling knives wipe accounts out.
2. **A crisis is where the biggest wealth transfers of a generation happen** — the best entries
   of the last 25 years (2002, March 2009, March 2020) all felt terrifying, and the leaders *out*
   of a crash are rarely the leaders that went in.

This file is the desk's operating manual for that regime. It sits above the normal rubric: when
`macro-regime.md` classifies the run as **Crisis / regime-shock**, this doctrine overrides the
default aggressiveness — defense first, then disciplined offense. The prime directive: **protect
the base so you're still solvent and liquid enough to buy the bottom.** The investor who survives
with dry powder wins the decade; the one who was fully invested and levered doesn't get to play.

---

## 1. Recognize you're in one (a pullback ≠ a regime shock)

Do not deploy the crisis playbook for a routine 3–8% dip. It is for a **regime shock** — a break
in the market's structure or a world-changing event. Tells (need several, not one):

- **VIX spikes hard** (>30 elevated, >40 panic, >60 = 2008/2020 tail) and stays there.
- **Credit blows out** — HY spreads widening fast, IG spreads moving, the *credit* market leading
  equities down. Credit is the early-warning system; watch it before the S&P confirms.
- **Cross-asset correlation → 1** — stocks, bonds, gold, crypto all selling together = forced
  de-leveraging / margin calls, not fundamental repricing.
- **Breadth washout / circuit breakers** — 90%-down days, new-low lists exploding, limit-down halts.
- **A regime catalyst** — a bursting bubble (valuations at an extreme unwinding), a credit/
  banking seizure, an exogenous shock (pandemic, war, oil embargo), or a policy rupture (tariff
  regime change, sovereign default). Name which kind — the *type* dictates the playbook (§5).
- **Funding stress** — dollar spiking, repo/rates dislocations, safe-haven bid in Treasuries/USD/gold.

If you see these, say so plainly at the top of the run and switch to this doctrine. A false alarm
costs a little opportunity; missing a real one costs the account.

## 2. The phase map — a crisis moves in stages, and each has its own job

Crises rhyme through four phases. Knowing which phase you're in is the whole game — the same buy
that's suicide in Phase 1 is the trade of the decade in Phase 3.

**Phase 1 — Recognition / de-risk (PROTECT THE BASE).** The break has started; most of the drop is
still ahead. Job = *stop the bleeding*, not bottom-fish.
- Cut leverage to zero. Margin is what turns a drawdown into a wipeout.
- Sell what's *broken or over-owned*: expensive/profitless story stocks, thesis-breaks, anything you
  only held on momentum. Raise cash aggressively — cash is a **position** here, the best hedge.
- Honor stops; do **not** average down into a confirmed downtrend ("catching a falling knife").
- Consider hedges for the core you keep: index puts, inverse exposure, trimming beta. Defined-risk
  (long puts) only — never sell naked premium into exploding vol.
- Rotate survivors toward quality/defensives (see house view: financials & healthcare in selloffs).

**Phase 2 — Panic / capitulation (START SCALING IN, IN TRANCHES).** Maximum fear; indiscriminate
selling; the fundamentals stop mattering for a while. Job = *begin building positions in quality,
slowly*, not to call the exact bottom.
- Watch for **capitulation tells**: a volume climax / selling exhaustion, a VIX blow-off top,
  breadth so bad it can't get worse, forced sellers done. You won't nail the low — accept that.
- **Scale in tranches** (e.g. thirds), buying *quality survivors* on the way down, not the zombies.
  The mentor method's "buy quality when forced/emotional flows set the price" is exactly this.
- Buy businesses that will **still exist and be stronger on the other side** — fortress balance
  sheet, positive cash flow or funded runway, real demand. Avoid the over-levered and the
  cash-burning; many *don't* come back (see the dot-com and 2008 lessons).
- Keep reserves. If you're all-in at the first capitulation, you can't buy the second, lower one.

**Phase 3 — Policy response / stabilization (ADD ON CONFIRMATION — don't fight the Fed/fiscal).**
The circuit-breaker is the authorities: a Fed bazooka (rate cuts, QE, backstops), fiscal stimulus,
a bailout, or a ceasefire. These have marked the bottom repeatedly (2008 TARP+QE → March 2009;
2020 "unlimited QE" + CARES Act → March 23, 2020). Job = *lean in as the regime turns*.
- Treat a decisive, credible policy response as a **major bottoming signal** — the "don't fight the
  Fed" moment. The turn is often violent and V-shaped; waiting for all-clear means missing 30–50%.
- Add to the quality positions started in Phase 2 as price reclaims key levels (the 30-day/50-day
  MA reclaim, higher-low structure). Confirmation over prediction.
- Distinguish a *real* backstop (open-ended, credible, targets the actual problem) from a *token* one.

**Phase 4 — New regime / new leadership (POSITION FOR THE NEXT CYCLE'S WINNERS).** The recovery is
underway and a new leadership emerges — **the leaders out are rarely the leaders that went in.**
Job = *own the new winners, not the last war's*.
- Post-2000: not the dot-coms — the survivors (AMZN) and the next platform (Web 2.0, mobile).
- Post-2008: not the banks — mega-cap tech / FAANG led the next decade.
- Post-2020: the digital-acceleration and mRNA winners first, then the reopening/inflation trade.
- Hand back to `thematic-waves.md`: the crisis *resets* the wave map — find the layer about to
  inflect in the *new* regime and be early. This is where the multi-baggers are minted.

## 3. Survival rules (protect the base — non-negotiable in a crisis)

1. **No leverage.** Ever, in a crisis. It's the single most common cause of permanent loss.
2. **Cash is a weapon, not a drag.** Target a *higher* cash allocation than normal; dry powder is
   what lets you buy the bottom. Being 40–60% cash at the start of a crash is a winning position.
3. **Liquidity first.** Only hold/buy names liquid enough to exit — spreads gap wide in a panic;
   illiquid microcaps become roach motels (ties to the rubric's liquidity gate, enforced harder).
4. **Size down, widen stops or use options.** Realized vol is 2–4×; a normal-sized position is now
   an oversized risk. Either cut size to keep dollar-risk constant, or express the view with
   defined-risk long options so a gap can't blow past your stop.
5. **Correlation is the hidden risk.** In a crisis everything you own is one trade. Don't stack
   correlated "cheap" names and think you're diversified.
6. **Survive to trade again.** Down 50% needs +100% to recover. The asymmetry of loss means the
   first job is not being forced to sell at the lows.

## 4. Offense rules (how crises make money)

1. **Barbell: safety + convexity.** Most of the book in cash/quality/defensives; a measured sleeve
   in *convex, asymmetric* bets (deeply mispriced quality, or defined-risk options with huge upside
   and capped loss). This is the rubric's asymmetric-bet sizing, dialed up for the opportunity.
2. **Buy quality on sale, in tranches, never all at once.** The list of "companies I'd own if they
   were 40% cheaper" is your Phase-2/3 shopping list — write it *before* the panic so you act on a
   plan, not adrenaline.
3. **Own the survivors, avoid the zombies.** A crash separates real businesses from stories. Fortress
   balance sheet + real cash flow + durable demand = survivor. Over-levered + cash-burning = avoid,
   however "cheap." A great company can still be a terrible stock at the wrong price *and* a broken
   company is not a bargain just because it fell 90%.
4. **Trade the direct beneficiaries for the duration of the shock.** Every crisis has names it
   *helps* (§5) — own them while the driver persists, with an exit plan for when it fades.
5. **Use options for convexity and to define risk.** Long calls/puts (or spreads) give leveraged
   upside with a known max loss — the right tool when the move is huge but the timing is uncertain.
   Respect that IV is expensive in a panic (you're paying up for vol); spreads help. Only within the
   account's option level (`options-playbook.md`).
6. **Separate the durable change from the temporary pull-forward** (a variant-perception discipline).
   Some crisis winners are *permanently* re-rated (structural acceleration); others just pulled a few
   quarters of demand forward and round-trip hard when the world normalizes. Getting this right is
   the difference between AMZN and Pets.com, between owning the trend and holding the bag.

## 5. Case studies — what worked, what to repeat, what to avoid

Each is a pattern to *pattern-match against*, not a prediction. Pull live data before acting;
history rhymes, it doesn't repeat.

### Case 1 — 2000 Dot-com bust (a valuation-bubble unwind)
- **What happened:** Nasdaq −78% (2000–2002). Profitless "eyeballs" story stocks went to zero;
  even great businesses (AMZN −~95%, later a 100-bagger) cratered because the *price* was insane.
- **What worked:** Selling extreme valuations *before* they broke; rotating to cash, value, and hard
  assets; refusing to "buy the dip" on names with no earnings and burning cash. Survivors were bought
  by those who waited for capitulation and demanded a real business + balance sheet. Shorting the
  zombies worked but was dangerous (timing/borrow).
- **Lesson (repeatable):** At a valuation extreme, **valuation is the catalyst** — profitless story
  stocks die, and a wonderful company at an absurd multiple is still a losing trade. The *survivors*
  of a popped bubble become the next decade's giants, but only the ones with real revenue and cash to
  outlast the winter. Modern tell: a hot theme where price has decoupled from any cash flow, funded by
  narrative and dilution.

### Case 2 — 2008 Global Financial Crisis (a credit / leverage seizure)
- **What happened:** S&P −57% peak-to-trough; a *credit* crisis (subprime → Lehman → counterparty
  panic) that froze the financial system. Leverage and opacity were the killers.
- **What worked:** (a) *Seeing it early in credit* — the few who read widening spreads and subprime
  data and bought protection (CDS) made fortunes (the "Big Short"). (b) *Buying the policy bottom* —
  March 2009, after TARP + the Fed's QE backstop, was a generational entry. (c) *Structured greed* —
  Buffett bought GS/GE via **preferreds + warrants**: income and downside protection *plus* upside,
  not naked common. Balance-sheet strength was survival.
- **Lesson (repeatable):** **Credit leads equity** — watch HY/IG spreads, leverage, and counterparty
  risk *before* the index cracks. In a solvency crisis, **balance sheet is everything**; avoid the
  over-levered even if "cheap." "Be greedy when others are fearful," but prefer *structured* downside
  protection where you can, and treat the decisive policy backstop as the buy signal.

### Case 3 — COVID-19 crash & recovery, 2020 (an exogenous shock + structural acceleration)
- **What happened:** Fastest-ever bear market (−34% in ~5 weeks) then the fastest recovery, on an
  unprecedented Fed + fiscal response (unlimited QE + CARES Act) that bottomed the market on ~Mar 23.
- **What worked:** (a) *Buying the policy bottom* once the Fed went "unlimited." (b) Owning what the
  shock **structurally accelerated** — digital/stay-at-home (ZM, SHOP, DOCU, NVDA, AMZN, PTON) and the
  vaccine race (MRNA, PFE, BNTX). (c) The later **reopening/reflation rotation** (energy, travel,
  small-cap value) as the world normalized.
- **Lesson (repeatable):** In an exogenous shock, ask **what does this permanently change?** and buy
  the *second-order* beneficiaries early. But **distinguish durable acceleration from pull-forward**:
  many 2020 darlings (ZM, PTON) round-tripped to below pre-COVID levels once behavior normalized —
  they pulled demand forward, they didn't re-rate forever. Speed of policy response set the bottom;
  the variant-perception edge was identifying which changes stuck.

### Case 4 — Trump trade war & social-media policy risk, 2025 (a policy-rupture / headline regime)
- **What happened:** Tariff escalation and **market-moving posts (X / Truth Social)** made policy
  headlines a dominant, un-chartable driver — sharp intraday air-pockets and rips on single
  announcements. A big dip is simultaneously a *risk* (to the exposed) and an *opportunity* (in names
  it doesn't actually impair).
- **What worked / the playbook:** (a) **Separate noise from cash-flow-changing policy** — most posts
  are noise; a real tariff/export rule that changes unit economics is not. Trade the latter, fade the
  former. (b) **Winners vs losers by exposure:** favor domestic supply chains, **reshoring**
  beneficiaries, pricing power, and policy-favored sectors (domestic energy, defense, industrials);
  reduce import-dependent retail and China-revenue-exposed names. (c) **Keep dry powder for headline
  dips** and buy quality that the tariff doesn't actually impair — the panic is indiscriminate. (d)
  **Don't hold naked short-dated risk into a known announcement**; use defined-risk options (spreads)
  around scheduled headline events; size for gap risk. (e) Treat unscheduled social-media shocks as
  *event risk that can't be stopped out* — hence smaller size and pre-defined "buy the overreaction"
  levels.
- **Lesson (repeatable):** In a headline-driven regime, **volatility ≠ impairment**. Have a written
  shopping list and buy-the-dip levels *in advance* so you exploit the air-pockets instead of being
  whipsawed by them, and always ask whether a given announcement actually changes future cash flows or
  just sentiment.

### Case 5 — Iran war, 2026 (a geopolitical / energy-supply shock)
- **The shape (forward-looking — pull live data, this is unfolding):** A Middle-East conflict transmits
  to markets mainly through **oil/energy** (supply-disruption or Strait-of-Hormuz risk premium), a
  **flight to safety** (gold, USD, Treasuries, defense), and second-order **inflation → Fed-path**
  effects if energy stays high. Broad equities usually sell off on the headline.
- **Playbook:** (a) **Direct beneficiaries for the duration:** energy/oil & gas, oilfield services,
  defense primes, tankers — own them while the supply/risk premium persists, with an exit for when it
  fades. (b) **Direct victims:** fuel-sensitive (airlines, transports, some consumer) and broad risk
  assets on the fear spike. (c) **The key question — contained or systemic?** History says most
  geopolitical selloffs are *short-lived and buyable* **unless** they cause a *lasting* oil shock or
  tip the economy toward recession. So separate the **transient fear** (buy the panic in quality names
  with no real exposure) from the **lasting economic transmission** (a sustained oil spike that lifts
  inflation, forces the Fed hawkish, and pressures multiples — a genuine regime change to respect).
  (d) **Hedge the tail** with defined-risk (energy calls / index puts) rather than guessing the
  headline. Size cautiously — war headlines gap markets with no warning.
- **Lesson (repeatable):** For a geopolitical shock, price the **economic transmission mechanism**
  (here: oil → inflation → Fed), not the scary headline. Own the direct beneficiaries while the driver
  lasts, buy the overreaction in the unaffected, and only treat it as a true regime change if it
  produces a durable macro shift (sustained energy inflation / recession), not a one-week fear spike.

## 6. How the desk uses this

1. **Trigger:** The Macro/Regime lens (`macro-regime.md`, `roles.md`) checks the §1 tells every run.
   If it classifies the regime as **Crisis / regime-shock**, it invokes this playbook and names the
   **crisis type** (bubble-unwind / credit-seizure / exogenous-shock / policy-rupture / geopolitical)
   and the **phase** (§2). Both go at the top of the report.
2. **Overlay on the rubric:** In a crisis, defense rules tighten — **no leverage, higher cash target,
   liquidity gate enforced harder, position sizes cut for elevated vol (or expressed as defined-risk
   options)**. The RR gate and variant-perception requirement still apply; the *opportunity* is sized
   via the rubric's asymmetric-bet sizing, in tranches, per the phase.
3. **Every crisis-era recommendation states:** the crisis type + phase, whether the name is a
   *survivor to accumulate*, a *direct beneficiary to rent*, or a *falling knife to avoid*, and — for
   any shock-winner — whether the tailwind is **durable** or a **pull-forward** (§4.6).
4. **Pre-commit the shopping list.** Maintain, in calm times, the "quality names I want and the crash
   prices I'd pay" list so Phase-2/3 buying executes a plan, not panic. Revisit it every crisis run.
5. **Hand back to `thematic-waves.md`** in Phase 4 to position for the *new* regime's leadership — the
   crisis resets the wave map, and the next multi-baggers are found there.
