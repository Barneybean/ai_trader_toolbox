# Secular Waves & the Multi-Bagger Hunt (where the real meat is)

The desk's biggest wins don't come from a 15% swing — they come from being **early in
the right layer of a generational buildout** and holding through the drawdowns. A
technology revolution doesn't arrive all at once; it **rolls through the value chain in
waves**, and capital **rotates to whatever the current bottleneck is**, then moves on when
that bottleneck's supply catches up or its valuation overshoots. The 20–40x is captured by
mapping the wave, anticipating the **next** constraint before consensus, buying the
under-covered beneficiary at its inflection, and sizing for asymmetry. This file is how.

This lens is applied **once per run** (like the Macro lens) to locate the wave front, and
it feeds candidates into the pipeline. It pairs with `references/variant-perception.md`
(the next-to-inflect layer is where non-consensus edge concentrates) and
`references/sector-playbooks.md` (the per-industry KPIs for each layer).

## How diffusion works (the mental model)

- **S-curve adoption.** Every technology diffuses slowly, then vertically (the steep
  middle), then saturates. The fat gains are entered *before* the steep part is consensus,
  and trimmed as saturation/overbuild nears.
- **Bottleneck rotation.** At each moment one layer is the binding constraint — demand
  overwhelms supply, so pricing/margins/orders spike there. Money floods in. When supply
  catches up (or the multiple blows out), capital **rotates to the next constraint down or
  up the stack.** Anticipating that rotation is the edge.
- **Picks-and-shovels first, platforms longest.** Early in a buildout, the enabling
  infrastructure/bottleneck suppliers inflect first (and can *round-trip* on overbuild).
  The **longest, most durable compounding** tends to accrue to the platforms/aggregators
  and category-definers that sit on top once the infra is built.
- **Overbuild → glut → shakeout → survivors.** Every buildout overshoots. The names that
  survive the glut and consolidate the market are where the second leg of returns lives.

## The AI buildout wave-map (rotation sequence)

Roughly in order of when each layer becomes the binding constraint. Track **where the wave
front is now, and what inflects next.** For each layer: the bottleneck, public
names/proxies, and the **inflection tell** (the dated signal its wave is turning).

1. **Accelerated compute — GPU.** The first bottleneck. (NVDA; AMD.) Tell: allocation,
   lead times, hyperscaler capex guides.
2. **Custom silicon / CPU / ASIC.** Hyperscaler in-house accelerators, networking silicon.
   (AVGO, MRVL; ARM.) Tell: custom-ASIC design wins, networking attach.
3. **Memory — HBM / DRAM / NAND.** Sold-out HBM, contract-locked pricing. (MU, SK Hynix,
   SNDK.) Tell: HBM sold-out announcements, LTAs, ASP inflection.
4. **Interconnect & optics — CPO, transceivers, silicon photonics, specialty glass/
   substrates.** As compute scales, moving data becomes the constraint. (Optical-component
   makers, silicon-photonics, glass-substrate/advanced-packaging suppliers.) Tell:
   co-packaged-optics design-ins, transceiver capacity, glass-substrate roadmaps.
5. **Power & electricity.** Data-center load outruns the grid — the hardest physical
   constraint. Generation, transformers/grid gear, gas turbines, and **nuclear** (SMR,
   uranium). (VST and IPPs; SMR/OKLO/NNE; utilities & grid-equipment names.) Tell: PPA
   signings with hyperscalers, interconnection queues, nuclear licensing steps.
6. **Materials & commodities.** The picks-and-shovels of the physical buildout: **copper**
   (electrification), **rare earths** (magnets/motors), cooling, uranium. Tell: pricing,
   supply constraints, offtake deals, policy (export controls).
7. **Compute capacity / data centers.** Real estate, cooling, networking. (DC REITs,
   cooling/thermal, networking.) Tell: pre-leasing, vacancy, capex.
8. **Model / platform layer.** Foundation models — mostly **private (OpenAI, Anthropic)**;
   public exposure via the hyperscaler platforms and their equity stakes/compute
   (MSFT, GOOG, AMZN, META). Tell: model capability jumps, token-cost curves, enterprise
   adoption, monetization.
9. **Application layer.** Software that monetizes AI in a vertical. (Application software
   with real AI revenue attach — separate the monetizers from the narrative.) Tell:
   AI-attributable revenue, pricing power, retention.
10. **Embodied AI / robotics / autonomy — the convergence.** Combines compute + power +
    models into the physical world (humanoids, autonomy, drones). The potential largest and
    latest wave. Tell: deployment scale, unit economics, order pipelines.

**Using the map:** don't just own the *current* hot layer (often already consensus/late) —
identify the layer whose bottleneck is *about to* bind, find its under-covered beneficiary
(often a second-order supplier or an orphan), and enter at the inflection tell. Copper/
power/optics were "boring" until compute made them the constraint — that's the pattern.

## History rhymes — the pattern library

Study prior revolutions for *who rose, who fell, and why* — the structure repeats.

- **Telegraph → telephone/telecom.** Western Union (the incumbent bottleneck) was displaced;
  AT&T built the durable network monopoly. *Lesson: the enabling incumbent can be the loser;
  the network/platform that captures recurring demand wins.*
- **Railroads / canals.** Massive overbuild, speculative mania, bust — but the survivors and
  the *logistics/land* beneficiaries compounded for decades. *Lesson: overbuild is normal;
  buy survivors and second-order winners after the shakeout.*
- **Electricity.** Enabling gear (GE) and utilities first; then the **application** wave
  (appliances, industrial productivity) created a second, larger set of winners. *Lesson:
  the rotation from enabling-infra to application is itself a trade.*
- **The Internet.** **Cisco / Intel / Sun / Nortel / JDSU** were the picks-and-shovels — they
  spiked spectacularly *and round-tripped*; Cisco never regained its 2000 peak despite the
  internet growing 1000x. Fiber was massively overbuilt (glut). Meanwhile the **platform/
  aggregators — Amazon, Google, later Meta —** compounded into the durable 20–40x+ winners.
  *Lesson: infra spikes first but can permanently round-trip on overbuild; the longest
  compounding is in the platforms/aggregators built on top. Timing and survivorship decide
  everything.*
- **Mobile / smartphone.** Apple (integrated platform), ARM/Qualcomm (IP/silicon), and an
  app-economy of new category winners (Uber, etc.). *Lesson: a platform shift mints new
  category-definers, not just faster versions of the old.*
- **EV / space.** **Tesla, SpaceX** created new S-curve categories and re-rated on execution
  through deep skepticism and drawdowns. *Lesson: category-definers deliver the biggest
  multiples but demand conviction through 50%+ drawdowns — position accordingly.*

**Distilled lessons the desk trades on:**
1. Infra/bottleneck names inflect first and can **round-trip** on overbuild — *ride the wave,
   don't marry it*; have an exit for when the bottleneck eases.
2. The **platform/aggregator/category-definer** layer compounds longest — that's the buy-and-
   hold multi-bagger core.
3. **Overbuild → glut → shakeout → survivors** is the cycle; the second leg of returns is in
   the survivors post-shakeout.
4. Adoption is an **S-curve** — enter before the steep middle is consensus; trim into
   saturation/euphoria.
5. The 20–40x requires being **early AND right AND surviving the drawdown** — which is a
   *sizing and conviction* problem, not just a stock-picking one.

## How the desk hunts and sizes the asymmetric bet

1. **Locate the wave front.** Once per run, state: which layer is the binding constraint now,
   which is about to inflect next, and which prior-wave layers are late/consensus (fade or
   avoid chasing).
2. **Find the under-covered beneficiary** of the next-to-inflect layer — usually a
   second-order supplier, a bottleneck component, or an orphan (`variant-perception.md`
   taxonomy #4/#5). Confirm with the sector playbook's KPIs.
3. **Time to the inflection tell,** not the narrative peak — orders, lead times, capex
   guides, PPA/design-win announcements. A great theme at a blown-out price is still a bad
   entry (the memory-names-mid-parabola lesson).
4. **Size for asymmetry, not certainty.** These are convex, high-variance bets: define a
   **small, bounded downside** and a **multi-x upside**, take a **small starter**, and
   **scale as the thesis confirms** (each catalyst that hits earns more size). A basket of
   several small asymmetric bets — where a couple win big — is the vehicle, never one all-in
   gamble. Respect the per-idea risk cap and the 25% concentration cap
   (`review-rubric.md`); the convexity, not oversizing, is what makes it work.
5. **Define invalidation and expect drawdowns.** Category-definers draw down 40–60% on the
   way up; pre-commit to the thesis-break conditions (not just price) so a drawdown doesn't
   shake you out of a winner or trap you in a loser.

**Guardrails.** Don't chase a wave that's already consensus and late — that's exactly where
the Cisco-2000 round-trip happens. Pair every theme with entry/valuation discipline. And
separate a **category-definer** (own through drawdowns) from a **bottleneck-spiker** (ride
and exit) — they demand opposite holding behavior.
