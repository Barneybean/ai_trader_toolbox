# Industry Mapping — layer a theme, price each layer, rank the reward:risk

The crowd buys a *theme* as one story ("humanoid robots", "AI", "obesity drugs") and pays one
narrative multiple across everything with the right words in its name. The edge is to **refuse the
single story**: decompose the theme into its **layers / supply chain**, price each layer
separately, and put capital where reward:risk is best — usually *not* the layer everyone means when
they say the theme's name. This is the structural front-end to `skills/edge/variant-perception.md`
and `skills/edge/thematic-waves.md` (which layer is *inflecting*); this skill is *how you carve the
industry up and grade the pieces.*

Use it for any "analyze the industry / sector / theme" request, and to place a single name inside
its industry before judging it.

---

## Step 1 — Cut the theme into layers (a value chain, not a list)

Map production from raw input → finished demand. For a hardware theme the spine is usually
**enabler / brain → integrator / body → components & materials → distribution / demand**. Name each
layer by the *job it does*, list the buyable tickers in each, and note who sells to whom (one
layer's customer capex is the next layer's revenue signal). If you can't say which layer a name
lives in, you don't understand the name yet.

> Worked cut (humanoid robots): **Brain** (compute/models/sim — NVDA, ARM, edge inference) →
> **Body/integrators** (whole-robot makers — TSLA/Optimus, Figure, Agility, Unitree) →
> **Components** (the bottleneck — dexterous hands, actuators/harmonic drives, rare-earth magnets,
> sensing/lidar) → **vehicles** (ETFs).

## Step 2 — Score each layer on three axes

For every layer, place it on:

- **Certainty** — how sure is it this layer gets paid *regardless of which competitor wins?*
  "Sells picks to everyone" (compute, magnets, actuators) scores high; "must win the end market"
  scores low.
- **Purity** — how much of a buy in this layer is *actually* the theme vs. something else riding
  along. NVDA is high-certainty but *low-purity* to robotics (≈1% of revenue) — you're mostly
  buying AI. Name the real exposure.
- **Elasticity** — upside if the theme works: small float + not-yet-priced + high theme-share =
  high elasticity; mega-cap where the theme is a rounding error = low.

The recurring pattern: **the safest layer has the lowest purity; the sexiest layer has the worst
reward:risk; the bottleneck layer is the most under-priced.** Rank layers by reward:risk, and say
plainly which layer you'd put new money in and why — often the boring middle-of-the-chain
bottleneck, not the layer whose name is on the theme.

## Step 3 — Proof over promise (who is *already collecting money?*)

Within a layer, separate **revenue already booked** from **narrative still being financed.** "Don't
read the promise on stage; read the cash already received." Signals that a business is real, not a
pitch: shipping product, signed commercial contracts (RaaS/offtake), booked backlog, a customer
already re-ordering. A layer collecting real money in a hype cycle is worth more than a layer
selling a deck — even at a higher headline multiple.

## Step 4 — Decompose the name: option vs. core

For a big name, split its price into **what you're really paying for** + **the theme as a free/cheap
option.** *"Buy NVDA = buy the AI leader, and the robot business is a lottery ticket you get on
top."* State it that way so the user knows whether they're buying a **pure** theme bet or a core
holding with **optionality**. A theme that's <5% of a company's value cannot drive the stock —
label it optionality, not a thesis.

## Step 5 — Anchor with a mature comparable (the yardstick)

Find one **validated, cash-generating** business in or adjacent to the theme and use it as a ruler
for the immature ones. *(ISRG — da Vinci, 20 years of installed base and cash flow — is the yardstick
that shows how far a pre-revenue humanoid "body" company is from mature.)* It calibrates both how big
the prize is and how much execution/time still separates the story from the proof.

## Step 6 — Read private marks as a thermometer

A large **private-market valuation reprices the whole public chain** — it sets the anchor suppliers
and comps get measured against. Treat a headline private round (e.g. Figure at $39B) as a
*thermometer for sentiment and a ceiling-setter*, not a tradable price; flag that it's a primary
mark, not a quote, and that it raises the bar (and the fragility) for everything downstream.

## Step 7 — Find the bottleneck via the BOM / cost stack

Pricing power sits where **cost and scarcity concentrate.** Decompose the unit economics (bill of
materials, or the equivalent cost stack for a service) and find the 1–2 line items that are the
biggest, hardest, and most supply-constrained. *(Hands ≈31% + joint actuators ≈30% = >half a
humanoid's BOM, and the hardest to make — so that's where the pricing power and the least-priced
edge sit.)* Whoever can mass-produce the bottleneck part holds the chain's throat.

## Step 8 — Check label vs. holdings (fake purity)

Before trusting *any* basket or "pure-play" claim, **verify the actual exposure matches the label.**
Many theme ETFs are a bag of decades-old adjacent stocks (a "robotics" fund that's mostly industrial
automation; an "AI" fund that's mega-cap tech). Read the real top holdings, weights, and revenue mix;
name the gap between the ticker's story and its guts. Same test on a single stock: does the segment
that carries the theme actually move the P&L?

## Output — the decision, ranked and tagged

Produce a **layer-ranked call**, each layer/name tagged **Buy / Accumulate · Hold/Core · Watch/Avoid**
with its axes (certainty/purity/elasticity), the proof, and the invalidation. Then an **actionable
matrix**: "want *sleep-well* → layer A; want *elasticity* → layer C; want a *basket* → fund X (+
don't double-own overlapping baskets)." Close with the **theme-level risks** (what falsifies the whole
chain — a production slip, a valuation reset, a supply/geopolitical chokepoint) and the **discipline**
(scale in, size for high implied vol, VC mindset on the un-proven layer). Every number dated and
sourced.

## Guardrails

- Layers, not vibes — if you can't place a name in a layer and price that layer, you haven't done the work.
- Certainty ≠ upside. The most-certain layer is usually the least elastic; say which the user is choosing.
- Proof > promise, but proof is priced — a real business at 200× can still be a bad *trade* (`skills/decision/review-rubric.md`).
- Private marks aren't quotes; theme ETFs aren't pure; a mega-cap's theme exposure is usually optionality. Verify all three.
- Pairs with `skills/edge/thematic-waves.md` (which layer inflects next), `skills/edge/smart-money.md` (who's funding/buying which layer), `skills/analysis/money-flow.md` (is the layer's leader under accumulation).
