# Crisis & Regime-Shock Playbook — Survive the Collapse, Hunt the Generational Trade

A **crisis** is not a normal tape: correlations go to 1 (diversification stops working),
liquidity evaporates, stops gap through their levels, the crowd sells indiscriminately — good
businesses and zombies together. Hold both truths at once:

1. **A crisis is the fastest way to be permanently impaired** — leverage, illiquidity, and knife-
   catching wipe accounts out.
2. **A crisis is where the biggest wealth transfers of a generation happen** — the best entries of
   the last 25 years (2002, March 2009, March 2020) all felt terrifying, and the leaders *out* of a
   crash are rarely the leaders that went in.

When `skills/analysis/macro-regime.md` classifies a run as **Crisis / regime-shock**, this
doctrine overrides default aggressiveness — defense first, then disciplined offense. Prime
directive: **protect the base so you're solvent and liquid enough to buy the bottom.** Survive
with dry powder and you win the decade; fully-invested and levered, you don't get to play.

---

## 1. Recognize you're in one (a pullback ≠ a regime shock)

Not for a routine 3–8% dip. For a **regime shock** — a break in market structure or a
world-changing event. Tells (need several, not one):

- **VIX spikes hard** (>30 elevated, >40 panic, >60 = 2008/2020 tail) and stays there.
- **Credit blows out** — HY spreads widening fast, IG moving, the *credit* market leading equities
  down. Credit is the early-warning system; watch it before the S&P confirms.
- **Cross-asset correlation → 1** — stocks, bonds, gold, crypto all selling together = forced
  de-leveraging / margin calls, not fundamental repricing.
- **Breadth washout / circuit breakers** — 90%-down days, exploding new-low lists, limit-down halts.
- **A regime catalyst** — a bursting bubble, a credit/banking seizure, an exogenous shock
  (pandemic, war, oil embargo), or a policy rupture (tariff regime change, sovereign default). Name
  the kind — the *type* dictates the playbook (§5).
- **Funding stress** — dollar spiking, repo/rates dislocations, safe-haven bid in Treasuries/USD/gold.

If you see these, say so plainly at the top of the run and switch to this doctrine. A false alarm
costs a little opportunity; missing a real one costs the account.

## 2. The phase map — a crisis moves in stages, each with its own job

Crises rhyme through four phases. Knowing which you're in is the whole game — the buy that's
suicide in Phase 1 is the trade of the decade in Phase 3.

**Phase 1 — Recognition / de-risk (PROTECT THE BASE).** Break started; most of the drop still
ahead. Job = *stop the bleeding*, not bottom-fish.
- Cut leverage to zero. Margin turns a drawdown into a wipeout.
- Sell what's *broken or over-owned*: expensive/profitless story stocks, thesis-breaks, momentum
  holds. Raise cash aggressively — cash is a **position**, the best hedge.
- Honor stops; do **not** average down into a confirmed downtrend.
- Hedge the core you keep: index puts, inverse exposure, trimming beta. Defined-risk (long puts)
  only — never sell naked premium into exploding vol.
- Rotate survivors to quality/defensives (house view: financials & healthcare in selloffs).

**Phase 2 — Panic / capitulation (START SCALING IN, IN TRANCHES).** Maximum fear, indiscriminate
selling, fundamentals stop mattering for a while. Job = *begin building quality, slowly*, not to
call the bottom.
- **Capitulation tells:** volume climax / selling exhaustion, VIX blow-off top, breadth so bad it
  can't worsen, forced sellers done. You won't nail the low — accept it.
- **Scale in tranches** (e.g. thirds), buying *quality survivors* on the way down, not zombies —
  the mentor method's "buy quality when forced/emotional flows set the price."
- Buy businesses that will **still exist and be stronger on the other side** — fortress balance
  sheet, positive cash flow or funded runway, real demand. Avoid the over-levered and cash-burning;
  many *don't* come back (dot-com and 2008 lessons).
- Keep reserves. All-in at the first capitulation and you can't buy the second, lower one.

**Phase 3 — Policy response / stabilization (ADD ON CONFIRMATION — don't fight the Fed/fiscal).**
The circuit-breaker is the authorities: a Fed bazooka (rate cuts, QE, backstops), fiscal stimulus,
a bailout, a ceasefire — these have marked the bottom repeatedly (2008 TARP+QE → March 2009; 2020
"unlimited QE" + CARES Act → March 23, 2020). Job = *lean in as the regime turns*.
- Treat a decisive, credible policy response as a **major bottoming signal** — the "don't fight the
  Fed" moment. The turn is often violent and V-shaped; waiting for all-clear means missing 30–50%.
- Add to Phase-2 quality as price reclaims key levels (30-/50-day MA reclaim, higher-low structure).
  Confirmation over prediction.
- Distinguish a *real* backstop (open-ended, credible, targets the actual problem) from a *token* one.

**Phase 4 — New regime / new leadership (POSITION FOR THE NEXT CYCLE'S WINNERS).** Recovery underway,
new leadership emerges — **the leaders out are rarely the leaders that went in.** Job = *own the new
winners, not the last war's*.
- Post-2000: not the dot-coms — the survivors (AMZN) and the next platform (Web 2.0, mobile).
- Post-2008: not the banks — mega-cap tech / FAANG led the next decade.
- Post-2020: digital-acceleration and mRNA winners first, then the reopening/inflation trade.
- Hand back to `skills/edge/thematic-waves.md`: the crisis *resets* the wave map — find the layer
  about to inflect in the *new* regime and be early. This is where the multi-baggers are minted.

## 3. Survival rules (protect the base — non-negotiable in a crisis)

1. **No leverage.** Ever, in a crisis. The single most common cause of permanent loss.
2. **Cash is a weapon, not a drag.** Target a *higher* cash allocation than normal; 40–60% cash at
   the start of a crash is a winning position.
3. **Liquidity first.** Only hold/buy names liquid enough to exit — spreads gap wide in a panic;
   illiquid microcaps become roach motels (rubric's liquidity gate, enforced harder).
4. **Size down, widen stops or use options.** Realized vol is 2–4×; a normal-sized position is now
   oversized. Cut size to keep dollar-risk constant, or express the view with defined-risk long
   options so a gap can't blow past your stop.
5. **Correlation is the hidden risk.** In a crisis everything you own is one trade. Don't stack
   correlated "cheap" names and think you're diversified.
6. **Survive to trade again.** Down 50% needs +100% to recover. The first job is not being forced to
   sell at the lows.

## 4. Offense rules (how crises make money)

1. **Barbell: safety + convexity.** Most of the book in cash/quality/defensives; a measured sleeve
   in *convex, asymmetric* bets (deeply mispriced quality, or defined-risk options with huge upside
   and capped loss). The rubric's asymmetric-bet sizing, dialed up.
2. **Buy quality on sale, in tranches, never all at once.** The "companies I'd own if 40% cheaper"
   list is your Phase-2/3 shopping list — write it *before* the panic so you act on a plan, not
   adrenaline.
3. **Own the survivors, avoid the zombies.** Fortress balance sheet + real cash flow + durable
   demand = survivor. Over-levered + cash-burning = avoid, however "cheap." A great company can be a
   terrible stock at the wrong price, and a broken one is no bargain just because it fell 90%.
4. **Trade the direct beneficiaries for the duration of the shock.** Every crisis has names it
   *helps* (§5) — own them while the driver persists, with an exit plan for when it fades.
5. **Use options for convexity and to define risk.** Long calls/puts (or spreads) give leveraged
   upside with known max loss — right when the move is huge but timing uncertain. IV is expensive in
   a panic; spreads help. Only within the account's option level (`skills/playbook/options.md`).
6. **Separate durable change from temporary pull-forward** (a variant-perception discipline). Some
   crisis winners are *permanently* re-rated (structural acceleration); others pulled a few quarters
   of demand forward and round-trip hard when the world normalizes. This is the difference between
   AMZN and Pets.com.

## 5. Case studies — what worked, what to repeat, what to avoid

Each is a pattern to *pattern-match against*, not a prediction. Pull live data before acting;
history rhymes, it doesn't repeat.

### Case 1 — 2000 Dot-com bust (a valuation-bubble unwind)
- **Happened:** Nasdaq −78% (2000–2002). Profitless "eyeballs" stocks went to zero; even great
  businesses (AMZN −~95%, later a 100-bagger) cratered because the *price* was insane.
- **Worked:** Selling extreme valuations *before* they broke; rotating to cash, value, hard assets;
  refusing to "buy the dip" on no-earnings, cash-burning names. Survivors went to those who waited
  for capitulation and demanded a real business + balance sheet. Shorting zombies worked but was
  dangerous (timing/borrow).
- **Lesson:** At a valuation extreme, **valuation is the catalyst** — profitless story stocks die,
  and a wonderful company at an absurd multiple is still a losing trade. The *survivors* become the
  next decade's giants, but only those with real revenue and cash to outlast the winter. Modern
  tell: a hot theme where price has decoupled from cash flow, funded by narrative and dilution.

### Case 2 — 2008 Global Financial Crisis (a credit / leverage seizure)
- **Happened:** S&P −57% peak-to-trough; a *credit* crisis (subprime → Lehman → counterparty panic)
  that froze the system. Leverage and opacity were the killers.
- **Worked:** (a) *Seeing it early in credit* — the few who read widening spreads and subprime data
  and bought protection (CDS) made fortunes (the "Big Short"). (b) *Buying the policy bottom* — March
  2009, after TARP + Fed QE backstop, was a generational entry. (c) *Structured greed* — Buffett
  bought GS/GE via **preferreds + warrants**: income + downside protection *plus* upside, not naked
  common. Balance-sheet strength was survival.
- **Lesson:** **Credit leads equity** — watch HY/IG spreads, leverage, counterparty risk *before*
  the index cracks. In a solvency crisis, **balance sheet is everything**; avoid the over-levered
  even if "cheap." "Be greedy when others are fearful," but prefer *structured* downside protection,
  and treat the decisive policy backstop as the buy signal.

### Case 3 — COVID-19 crash & recovery, 2020 (exogenous shock + structural acceleration)
- **Happened:** Fastest-ever bear market (−34% in ~5 weeks) then the fastest recovery, on an
  unprecedented Fed + fiscal response (unlimited QE + CARES Act) that bottomed the market ~Mar 23.
- **Worked:** (a) *Buying the policy bottom* once the Fed went "unlimited." (b) Owning what the shock
  **structurally accelerated** — digital/stay-at-home (ZM, SHOP, DOCU, NVDA, AMZN, PTON) and the
  vaccine race (MRNA, PFE, BNTX). (c) The later **reopening/reflation rotation** (energy, travel,
  small-cap value).
- **Lesson:** In an exogenous shock, ask **what does this permanently change?** and buy the
  *second-order* beneficiaries early. But **distinguish durable acceleration from pull-forward**:
  many 2020 darlings (ZM, PTON) round-tripped below pre-COVID levels once behavior normalized — they
  pulled demand forward, didn't re-rate forever. Policy speed set the bottom; the variant-perception
  edge was identifying which changes stuck.

### Case 4 — Trump trade war & social-media policy risk, 2025 (a policy-rupture / headline regime)
- **Happened:** Tariff escalation and **market-moving posts (X / Truth Social)** made policy
  headlines a dominant, un-chartable driver — sharp intraday air-pockets and rips on single
  announcements. A big dip is at once a *risk* (to the exposed) and an *opportunity* (in names it
  doesn't impair).
- **Playbook:** (a) **Separate noise from cash-flow-changing policy** — most posts are noise; a real
  tariff/export rule that changes unit economics is not. Trade the latter, fade the former. (b)
  **Winners vs losers by exposure:** favor domestic supply chains, **reshoring**, pricing power, and
  policy-favored sectors (domestic energy, defense, industrials); reduce import-dependent retail and
  China-revenue-exposed names. (c) **Keep dry powder for headline dips**; buy quality the tariff
  doesn't impair. (d) **Don't hold naked short-dated risk into a known announcement**; use
  defined-risk options (spreads) around scheduled events; size for gap risk. (e) Treat unscheduled
  social-media shocks as *event risk that can't be stopped out* — smaller size, pre-defined "buy the
  overreaction" levels.
- **Lesson:** In a headline-driven regime, **volatility ≠ impairment**. Have a written shopping list
  and buy-the-dip levels *in advance* to exploit air-pockets instead of being whipsawed, and always
  ask whether an announcement changes future cash flows or just sentiment.

### Case 5 — Iran war, 2026 (a geopolitical / energy-supply shock)
- **The shape (forward-looking — pull live data, this is unfolding):** A Middle-East conflict
  transmits mainly through **oil/energy** (supply-disruption or Strait-of-Hormuz risk premium), a
  **flight to safety** (gold, USD, Treasuries, defense), and second-order **inflation → Fed-path**
  effects if energy stays high. Broad equities usually sell off on the headline.
- **Playbook:** (a) **Direct beneficiaries for the duration:** energy/oil & gas, oilfield services,
  defense primes, tankers — own them while the supply/risk premium persists, with an exit for when it
  fades. (b) **Direct victims:** fuel-sensitive (airlines, transports, some consumer) and broad risk
  assets on the fear spike. (c) **Contained or systemic?** Most geopolitical selloffs are
  *short-lived and buyable* **unless** they cause a *lasting* oil shock or tip the economy toward
  recession. Separate **transient fear** (buy the panic in quality names with no real exposure) from
  **lasting economic transmission** (a sustained oil spike that lifts inflation, forces the Fed
  hawkish, pressures multiples — a genuine regime change to respect). (d) **Hedge the tail** with
  defined-risk (energy calls / index puts) rather than guessing the headline. Size cautiously — war
  headlines gap markets with no warning.
- **Lesson:** For a geopolitical shock, price the **economic transmission mechanism** (here: oil →
  inflation → Fed), not the scary headline. Own the direct beneficiaries while the driver lasts, buy
  the overreaction in the unaffected, and treat it as a true regime change only if it produces a
  durable macro shift (sustained energy inflation / recession), not a one-week fear spike.

## 6. How the desk uses this

1. **Trigger:** The Macro/Regime lens (`skills/analysis/macro-regime.md`,
   `skills/decision/roles.md`) checks the §1 tells every run. On **Crisis / regime-shock**, it
   invokes this playbook and names the **crisis type** (bubble-unwind / credit-seizure /
   exogenous-shock / policy-rupture / geopolitical) and the **phase** (§2). Both go at the top of the
   report.
2. **Overlay on the rubric:** defense rules tighten — **no leverage, higher cash target, liquidity
   gate enforced harder, position sizes cut for elevated vol (or expressed as defined-risk
   options)**. The RR gate and variant-perception requirement still apply; the *opportunity* is sized
   via the rubric's asymmetric-bet sizing, in tranches, per the phase.
3. **Every crisis-era rec states:** the crisis type + phase, whether the name is a *survivor to
   accumulate*, a *direct beneficiary to rent*, or a *falling knife to avoid*, and — for any
   shock-winner — whether the tailwind is **durable** or a **pull-forward** (§4.6).
4. **Pre-commit the shopping list.** Maintain, in calm times, the "quality names I want and the crash
   prices I'd pay" list so Phase-2/3 buying executes a plan, not panic. Revisit every crisis run.
5. **Hand back to `skills/edge/thematic-waves.md`** in Phase 4 to position for the *new* regime's
   leadership — the crisis resets the wave map, and the next multi-baggers are found there.
