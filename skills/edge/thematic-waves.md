# Secular Waves & the Multi-Bagger Hunt (where the real meat is)

The biggest wins don't come from a 15% swing — they come from being **early in the right layer of a generational buildout** and holding through drawdowns. A technology revolution **rolls through the value chain in waves**; capital **rotates to whatever the current bottleneck is**, then moves on as that bottleneck's supply catches up or its valuation overshoots. The 20–40x is captured by mapping the wave, anticipating the **next** constraint before consensus, buying the under-covered beneficiary at its inflection, and sizing for asymmetry.

Apply this lens **once per run** (like the Macro lens) to locate the wave front and feed candidates into the pipeline. Pairs with `skills/edge/variant-perception.md` (the next-to-inflect layer is where non-consensus edge concentrates) and `skills/analysis/sector-playbooks.md` (per-industry KPIs per layer).

## How diffusion works (the mental model)

- **S-curve adoption.** Every technology diffuses slowly, then vertically (the steep middle), then saturates. Enter *before* the steep part is consensus; trim as saturation/overbuild nears.
- **Bottleneck rotation.** At each moment one layer is the binding constraint — demand overwhelms supply, so pricing/margins/orders spike there and money floods in. When supply catches up (or the multiple blows out), capital **rotates to the next constraint up or down the stack.** Anticipating that rotation is the edge.
- **Picks-and-shovels first, platforms longest.** Early on, infrastructure/bottleneck suppliers inflect first (and can *round-trip* on overbuild). The **longest, most durable compounding** accrues to the platforms/aggregators and category-definers on top once the infra is built.
- **Overbuild → glut → shakeout → survivors.** Every buildout overshoots. Survivors that consolidate the market hold the second leg of returns.

## The AI buildout wave-map (rotation sequence)

Roughly ordered by when each layer becomes the binding constraint. Track **where the wave front is now, and what inflects next.** Each layer: bottleneck, public names/proxies, **inflection tell** (the dated signal its wave is turning).

1. **Accelerated compute — GPU.** First bottleneck. (NVDA; AMD.) Tell: allocation, lead times, hyperscaler capex guides.
2. **Custom silicon / CPU / ASIC.** Hyperscaler in-house accelerators, networking silicon. (AVGO, MRVL; ARM.) Tell: custom-ASIC design wins, networking attach.
3. **Memory — HBM / DRAM / NAND.** Sold-out HBM, contract-locked pricing. (MU, SK Hynix, SNDK.) Tell: HBM sold-out announcements, LTAs, ASP inflection.
4. **Interconnect & optics — CPO, transceivers, silicon photonics, specialty glass/substrates.** As compute scales, moving data becomes the constraint. (Optical-component, silicon-photonics, glass-substrate/advanced-packaging suppliers.) Tell: co-packaged-optics design-ins, transceiver capacity, glass-substrate roadmaps.
5. **Power & electricity.** Data-center load outruns the grid — the hardest physical constraint. Generation, transformers/grid gear, gas turbines, and **nuclear** (SMR, uranium). (VST and IPPs; SMR/OKLO/NNE; utilities & grid-equipment.) Tell: PPA signings with hyperscalers, interconnection queues, nuclear licensing steps.
6. **Materials & commodities.** Picks-and-shovels of the physical buildout: **copper** (electrification), **rare earths** (magnets/motors), cooling, uranium. Tell: pricing, supply constraints, offtake deals, policy (export controls).
7. **Compute capacity / data centers.** Real estate, cooling, networking. (DC REITs, cooling/thermal, networking.) Tell: pre-leasing, vacancy, capex.
8. **Model / platform layer.** Foundation models — mostly **private (OpenAI, Anthropic)**; public exposure via hyperscaler platforms and their equity stakes/compute (MSFT, GOOG, AMZN, META). Tell: model capability jumps, token-cost curves, enterprise adoption, monetization.
9. **Application layer.** Software that monetizes AI in a vertical. (App software with real AI revenue attach — separate monetizers from narrative.) Tell: AI-attributable revenue, pricing power, retention.
10. **Embodied AI / robotics / autonomy — the convergence.** Compute + power + models into the physical world (humanoids, autonomy, drones). Potentially the largest and latest wave. Tell: deployment scale, unit economics, order pipelines.

**Using the map:** don't just own the *current* hot layer (often already consensus/late) — identify the layer whose bottleneck is *about to* bind, find its under-covered beneficiary (often a second-order supplier or orphan), and enter at the inflection tell. Copper/power/optics were "boring" until compute made them the constraint — that's the pattern.

## History rhymes — the pattern library

Study prior revolutions for *who rose, who fell, and why* — the structure repeats.

- **Telegraph → telephone/telecom.** Western Union (incumbent bottleneck) was displaced; AT&T built the durable network monopoly. *Lesson: the enabling incumbent can be the loser; the network/platform capturing recurring demand wins.*
- **Railroads / canals.** Massive overbuild, mania, bust — but survivors and *logistics/land* beneficiaries compounded for decades. *Lesson: overbuild is normal; buy survivors and second-order winners after the shakeout.*
- **Electricity.** Enabling gear (GE) and utilities first; then the **application** wave (appliances, industrial productivity) created a second, larger set of winners. *Lesson: the rotation from enabling-infra to application is itself a trade.*
- **The Internet.** **Cisco / Intel / Sun / Nortel / JDSU** were the picks-and-shovels — they spiked spectacularly *and round-tripped*; Cisco never regained its 2000 peak despite the internet growing 1000x. Fiber was massively overbuilt (glut). The **platform/aggregators — Amazon, Google, later Meta —** compounded into the durable 20–40x+ winners. *Lesson: infra spikes first but can permanently round-trip on overbuild; the longest compounding is in the platforms on top. Timing and survivorship decide everything.*
- **Mobile / smartphone.** Apple (integrated platform), ARM/Qualcomm (IP/silicon), and an app-economy of new category winners (Uber, etc.). *Lesson: a platform shift mints new category-definers, not just faster versions of the old.*
- **EV / space.** **Tesla, SpaceX** created new S-curve categories and re-rated on execution through deep skepticism and drawdowns. *Lesson: category-definers deliver the biggest multiples but demand conviction through 50%+ drawdowns — position accordingly.*

**Distilled lessons the desk trades on:**
1. Infra/bottleneck names inflect first and can **round-trip** on overbuild — *ride the wave, don't marry it*; have an exit for when the bottleneck eases.
2. The **platform/aggregator/category-definer** layer compounds longest — the buy-and-hold multi-bagger core.
3. **Overbuild → glut → shakeout → survivors** is the cycle; the second leg of returns is in the survivors post-shakeout.
4. Adoption is an **S-curve** — enter before the steep middle is consensus; trim into saturation/euphoria.
5. The 20–40x requires being **early AND right AND surviving the drawdown** — a *sizing and conviction* problem, not just stock-picking.

## How the desk hunts and sizes the asymmetric bet

1. **Locate the wave front.** Once per run, state: which layer is the binding constraint now, which is about to inflect next, which prior-wave layers are late/consensus (fade or don't chase).
2. **Find the under-covered beneficiary** of the next-to-inflect layer — usually a second-order supplier, bottleneck component, or orphan (`skills/edge/variant-perception.md` taxonomy #4/#5). Confirm with the sector playbook's KPIs.
3. **Time to the inflection tell,** not the narrative peak — orders, lead times, capex guides, PPA/design-win announcements. A great theme at a blown-out price is a bad entry (the memory-names-mid-parabola lesson).
4. **Size for asymmetry, not certainty.** These are convex, high-variance bets: define a **small, bounded downside** and **multi-x upside**, take a **small starter**, and **scale as the thesis confirms** (each catalyst that hits earns more size). A basket of several small asymmetric bets — where a couple win big — is the vehicle, never one all-in gamble. Respect the per-idea risk cap and the 25% concentration cap (`skills/decision/review-rubric.md`); the convexity, not oversizing, makes it work.
5. **Define invalidation and expect drawdowns.** Category-definers draw down 40–60% on the way up; pre-commit to thesis-break conditions (not just price) so a drawdown doesn't shake you out of a winner or trap you in a loser.

**Guardrails.** Don't chase a wave that's already consensus and late — exactly where the Cisco-2000 round-trip happens. Pair every theme with entry/valuation discipline. Separate a **category-definer** (own through drawdowns) from a **bottleneck-spiker** (ride and exit) — they demand opposite holding behavior.
